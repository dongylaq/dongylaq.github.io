Identical Particles and Configuration Space

Identical particles in quantum mechanics refer to an indistinguishbility, namely, we cannot label those particles and say, for example, specifically one occupies given position.

Example (Identical Particles, cf. p31)

Suppose we have two identical particles in a system, there is no meaning to saying that particle one is at position x1x_1x​1​​ and two is at position x2x_2x​2​​, interns of ket language ∣x1,x2⟩|x_1, x_2\rangle∣x​1​​,x​2​​⟩. We can only say there are particles at both x1x_1x​1​​ and x2x_2x​2​​.

For simplicity, we always assume particles cannot overlap, namely, xi≠xj,i≠jx_i \neq x_j,\ i \neq jx​i​​≠x​j​​,i≠j. Let's define configuration space of NNN identical particles as C=(RNd−Δ)/SN\mathcal{C} = (\mathbb{R}^{Nd} - \Delta) / S_NC=(R​Nd​​−Δ)/S​N​​, where Δ\DeltaΔ is the set that at least two particles occupy the same position and the modulus wrt permutation group SNS_NS​N​​ stands for the indistinguishability. What we want to know is the equivalent classes, fundamental group, defined on configuration space and their representations to make consistency of composition of propagators.

Let's first rewrite the path integral wrt foundamental group GGG,

\langle x_f | U(t_f, t_i) | x_i \rangle = \mathcal{N} \sum_{g \in G} \rho(g) \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow f}\in g} e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow j}]/\hbar}\label{Eq:PIFG}

where ρ\rhoρ is a representation of GGG. Then, the composition of path integral requires the following identity.

\begin{split} \sum_{g \in G} \rho(g) \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow f}\in g} e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow j}]/\hbar} &= \int d x_m \left( \sum_{g_1 \in G} \rho(g_1) \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow m}\in g_1} e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow m}]/\hbar} \right)\left( \sum_{g_2 \in G} \rho(g_2) \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{m\rightarrow f}\in g_1} e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{m\rightarrow j}]/\hbar} \right)\\ &= \int d x_m \sum_{g_1, g_2 \in G} \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow m} \in g_1} \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{m\rightarrow j} \in g_2} \rho(g_1)\rho(g_2) e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow j}]/\hbar}\\ &= \int d x_m \sum_{g_1, g_2 \in G} \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow m} \in g_1} \sum_{\mathrm{path}_{m\rightarrow j} \in g_2} \rho(g_1g_2) e^{i S[\mathrm{path}_{i\rightarrow j}]/\hbar} \end{split}

The last equality follows that composing two path segaments in class g1g_1g​1​​ and g2g_2g​2​​ results in a longer path in class g1g2g_1 g_2g​1​​g​2​​. That implies that ρ(g1g2)=ρ(g1)ρ(g2)\rho(g_1 g_2) = \rho(g_1) \rho(g_2)ρ(g​1​​g​2​​)=ρ(g​1​​)ρ(g​2​​) which is nothing but the property of a representation.

Abelian Representation

Let's first consider the case of one dimensional representation, ρ:G→S1\rho : G \rightarrow S^1ρ:G→S​1​​. This is called abelian because of the comutativity of complex numbers. We will talk about nonabelian situation in which the representation is matrices which are generally not commutative.

3+1 Dimensions

In 3+1 D, the fundamental group through configuration space is the symmetric group SNS_NS​N​​, which has only two possible 1D representations.

  • Trivial representation: ρ(g)=1,∀g∈G\rho(g) = 1, \forall g \in Gρ(g)=1,∀g∈G. This corresponds to bosons.
  • Alternating representation: ρ(g)=1or−1\rho(g) = 1\ \mathrm{or}\ -1ρ(g)=1or−1 depending on the even or odd number of exchanges. This corresponds to fermions.
2+1 Dimensions

In 2+1 D, the group GGG is the braid group BNB_NB​N​​ whose 1D representation is described as ρ(g)=eiθW(g)\rho(g) = e^{i \theta W(g)}ρ(g)=e​iθW(g)​​, where θ\thetaθ is a parameter and WWW is the winding number of braid ggg.

Definition (Winding Number)

Winding number is a braid invariant, defined as W=(#overcrossing)−(#undercrossing)W = (\# \mathrm{overcrossing}) - (\# \mathrm{undercrossing})W=(#overcrossing)−(#undercrossing). The convention of crossing is that the clockwise exchange from top view is positive, overcrossing.

Therefore, a clockwise exchange accumulates a phase of eiθe^{i \theta}e​iθ​​.

  • θ=0\theta = 0θ=0: Bosons.
  • θ=π\theta = \piθ=π: Fermions.
  • Other value of θ\thetaθ: Anyons, fractional statistics

Nonabelian Representation

In quantum mechanics, high dimensional representation corresponds to degeneracies. Suppose even for fixed set of position xxx, we have a system still remains a MMM-fold degenerate subspace spaned by {∣n;x⟩:n=1,⋯,M}\{|n ; x\rangle : n = 1, \cdots, M \}{∣n;x⟩:n=1,⋯,M}. Then, the system state in degenerate subspace can be expressed as a linear combination of those bases, ∣ψ⟩=∑nAn∣n;x⟩|\psi\rangle = \sum_n A_n | n; x \rangle∣ψ⟩=∑​n​​A​n​​∣n;x⟩, which means the degenerate system can be characterized by a vector in CM\mathbb{C}^MC​M​​. Now, the matrix element of propagator indeed depends with degeneracy label nnn, [⟨xf∣U(tf,ti)∣xi⟩]n,n′=⟨n;xf∣U(tf,ti)∣n′;xi⟩[\langle x_f | U(t_f, t_i) | x_i \rangle]_{n, n^\prime} = \langle n; x_f | U(t_f, t_i) | n^\prime; x_i\rangle[⟨x​f​​∣U(t​f​​,t​i​​)∣x​i​​⟩]​n,n​′​​​​=⟨n;x​f​​∣U(t​f​​,t​i​​)∣n​′​​;x​i​​⟩. Thus, we now have a high dimensional representation of braid group, ρ:G→U(M)\rho : G \rightarrow \mathrm{U}(M)ρ:G→U(M).
